Why Pressure Canning Is Non-Negotiable for Off-Grid Food Storage

Low-acid foods — all vegetables, meat, beans, fish — must be pressure canned. Water bath canning cannot reach 240°F, the temperature required to kill Clostridium botulinum spores that cause botulism. A pressure canner is not optional for comprehensive food storage; it’s required for the majority of what a homestead garden produces.

The All American 921 is the most respected pressure canner among serious homesteaders, and the reason comes down to one design choice: the seal.

The No-Gasket Metal-to-Metal Seal

Most pressure canners use a rubber gasket that degrades and must be replaced periodically. The All American uses a precision-machined metal-to-metal seal: the lid screws directly onto the pot body with no rubber gasket between them. The result is a canner with no consumable sealing parts — canners purchased 30–40 years ago are still in active use.

This is the fundamental reason the All American commands a premium. It is a one-time purchase.

What You Can and Can’t Can

  • Pressure can: All vegetables (green beans, corn, potatoes, beets, carrots), all meats (chicken, beef, venison, pork), dried beans, soups, stews, fish, game
  • Water bath only: High-acid foods — tomatoes (with added acid), jams, jellies, pickles, applesauce
  • Never can: Dairy, starch/flour-thickened products, pasta, rice, eggs

Capacity: 19 wide-mouth pint jars or 7 wide-mouth quart jars per batch.

Always Use USDA-Tested Recipes

Processing times and pressures are food safety requirements, not suggestions. Always follow USDA tested and approved canning recipes — do not reduce processing time or pressure from published values. The All American is the tool; proper technique is your responsibility.
