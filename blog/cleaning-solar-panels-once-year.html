Cleaning Solar Panels Once a Year: The System We Use

We clean our solar panels once per year — in early spring after the winter accumulation has finished. Our 2.4 kW array sits at a 35-degree tilt, faces south, and is 20 feet from our well. The annual cleaning takes 45 minutes and restores our panels to full output.

By Jake Mercer · Off-Grid Homesteader & Former Engineer

In This Article

The Data: What Dirty Panels Cost Us

Before designing our cleaning system, we measured the impact of dirty panels. We tracked production for three weeks with no cleaning, then cleaned and tracked again:

ConditionDaily Production (March)LossAnnual Impact
Clean9.1 kWhBaseline$0
Light dust8.2 kWh10%$45/yr
Moderate dirt7.3-7.8 kWh15-20%$70-90/yr
Heavy dirt6.4-6.8 kWh25-30%$110-135/yr

In our case (2.4 kW array), the difference between clean and dirty in March is roughly 0.9 kWh per day, or 27 kWh per month (30-day month). At $0.14/kWh, that is $3.78 per month — or $45 per year. The numbers are smaller than some websites claim, but they are real.

Why the loss matters more in summer: our panels produce 12-14 kWh per day in June (clean) vs. 8-9 kWh (dirty). That 30% loss in summer costs us $5.60/month vs. $3.78/month in spring.

Why Dirty Panels Lose Power

Here is the technical explanation: solar panels work when photons from sunlight knock electrons loose from the silicon atoms. The electrons flow through the cell and create current. Three things block this:

  • Physical shading: Dirt blocks photons from reaching the cells. Even a thin layer cuts the available photons.
  • Changed light angle: Dirt scatters incoming light, changing the angle at which photons hit the cells. Some bounce off entirely.
  • Hot spots: Dirty areas heat up faster than clean areas. Hot panels are less efficient. Every 1°C above 25°C (77°F) reduces efficiency by 0.5%. A hot, dirty panel loses twice.

The most damaging type of dirt is not dust. It is bird droppings. A small area of droppings can create a hot spot that permanently damages a cell if left for weeks. Clean droppings immediately.

When to Clean: Timing Matters

We clean once per year in early spring — late March or early April in our zone 6b. The timing is specific:

Why spring:

  1. Winter accumulation: Snow, ice, and winter debris are done accumulating. Cleaning after winter captures the full winter-to-spring period.
  2. Before summer peak: Cleaning in April means we hit the summer production peak (June-August) with clean panels.
  3. Weather window: Spring temperatures in the 50-70°F range are ideal for cleaning. Summer heat makes it uncomfortable and can cause thermal shock to the panels if you spray cold water on hot glass.

Do not clean in winter — the risk of refreezing and ice damage exceeds the benefit.

Our Cleaning System

We clean once per year in early spring — but the tools and technique we use apply whenever you clean.

Tools We Use

We have simplified the cleaning process to three tools:

  • Soft brush: A car-washing brush with telescoping handle. Foam or lamb's wool head, no bristles.
  • Garden hose: No pressure washer needed. We tested pressurized vs. gentle — no difference in results.
  • Mild dish soap: Dawn or similar — one tablespoon per gallon.

Total cost: $20 for brush + soap you already have. No specialized solar cleaning solutions needed.

What NOT to Use

  • Steel wool or abrasive pads: Will scratch the glass. Micro-scratches reduce efficiency permanently.
  • High-pressure washer: Can break seals and force water underneath the frame. Keep 4+ feet distance if you must.
  • Ammonia or harsh chemicals: Can damage the anti-reflective coating on the glass.
  • Hot water on cold panels: Thermal shock can crack the glass. Wait for panels to reach ambient temperature first.
  • Wiper blades: Never drag a blade across panels. They will scratch.

Do not use specialized solar cleaning solutions. They are not necessary and add cost.

The Technique

Step-by-step:

  1. Pre-wet: Spray panels with water to loosen debris. Do not use high pressure — just enough to wet the surface.
  2. Apply soapy water: Add one tablespoon of dish soap per gallon. Apply with the brush or a pump sprayer.
  3. Scrub gently: Use the soft brush in long strokes from top to bottom. Do not scrub aggressively — the debris is already loose.
  4. Rinse: Rinse from top to bottom with clean water. Start at the top edge and let the water sheet down — this prevents streaking.
  5. Dry: Allow to air dry or wipe with a clean soft cloth. If you have hard water, wipe to prevent mineral deposits.

Total time for our 2.4 kW array: 45 minutes.

What About Rain?

Some websites claim rain cleans your panels. In our experience, light rain just spreads the dirt. Heavy rain helps, but our panels are tilted at 35 degrees — not steep enough for rain to wash effectively. Unless you live in an area with frequent heavy rain, plan on manual cleaning.

Additional Considerations

Ground-Mount vs. Roof-Mount

Our experience is with ground-mounted panels. Roof-mounted panels are harder to access safely and may need professional cleaning. If you are on a roof:

  • Do not walk on panels — they can crack
  • Use a professional service if you are not comfortable with roof work
  • The cleaning principle is the same, but the access is different

Tilt Angle

Panels at higher tilt angles (greater than 30 degrees) self-clean better in rain. Ours at 35 degrees clean themselves more than panels at 20-25 degrees. But even at 35 degrees, we still clean manually — rain does not remove bird droppings or sticky pollen.

Shading from Trees

If you have trees nearby, expect more debris. A panel under a tree drops more leaves, sap, and pollen than an exposed panel. Consider tree trimming as part of your annual maintenance.

Measuring Cleanliness

You do not need fancy equipment to measure panel output — your inverter or monitoring system shows your production. Compare daily output before and after cleaning.

For a more precise measurement, you can use:

  • I-V curve tracer: Measures panel performance curve. Expensive and overkill for most users.
  • Infrared camera: Shows hot spots from blocked cells. Interesting but not necessary for regular maintenance.
  • Simple comparison: Just look at your daily kWh — if it increases 10% after cleaning, your panels were dirty.

We use the simple comparison method and track our daily production in a spreadsheet.

Our Annual Calendar

This is when we do what:

  • March 15: Inspect panels. Check for damage, heavy soiling, or shading from winter debris (fallen branches).
  • March 30-April 1: Clean panels. Timing depends on weather – wait for a warm day above 50°F.
  • Monthly: Check for shading from new tree growth. Trim as needed.
  • Monthly: Review production data. If production drops more than 10% from expected, investigate.

Common Mistakes

Too Frequent Cleaning

Cleaning more than once per year is unnecessary for most locations. Unless you are in a high-debris area (dust storm, heavy pollen, construction), once per year is enough.

Using Wrong Materials

Never use:

  • Steel wool or abrasive pads: Will scratch the glass
  • High-pressure washer: Can break seals and force water underneath
  • Ammonia or harsh chemicals: Can damage the anti-reflective coating
  • Hot water on cold panels: Thermal shock can crack the glass

Cleaning at Wrong Time

Do not clean in:

  • Direct midday sun: The water dries too quickly, leaving streaks
  • Cold weather below 50°F: Risk of ice on the panels after cleaning
  • Hot panels: Thermal shock risk

Ideal conditions: overcast morning, temperature 50-70°F.

Getting Started

You need:

  1. Soft brush with handle ($15-20): Car-wash brush works fine
  2. Garden hose ($0): Already in your yard
  3. Dish soap ($0): Already in your kitchen
  4. Ladder if needed ($0): For roof-mounted panels, may need a ladder

Total cost: $15-20, plus any ladder equipment you may already have.
