The Best Websites for Weather Information: Our Tested List

We have tested weather websites continuously for 18 months. We use weather data to time our solar production, plan water collection, anticipate food preservation needs, and make go/no-go decisions on off-grid work.

By Jake Mercer · Off-Grid Homesteader & Former Engineer

In This Article

Our Tested Top Picks

1. Windy.com (Overall Best)

What it does: Interactive weather map with highly accurate forecast models, wind visualization, and real-time data.

Why we use it: Windy is the single most useful weather website we have found. The interface shows animated wind patterns, temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover overlaid on an interactive map. You can select multiple forecast models (ECMWF, GFS, ICON) and compare them side by side.

Key features:

  • Forecast models: Switch between ECMWF (most accurate overall), GFS (US-centric), ICON (European), and others
  • Wind animation: See wind patterns develop over time — critical for storm tracking
  • Weather webcams: Overlay shows thousands of user-contributed webcams worldwide
  • Alerts: Custom alerts for specific conditions (wind speed, temperature, precipitation)

The free tier includes all core features. The paid tier ($13/year) adds 5-day forecast at 1-hour intervals, longer forecast horizon, and no ads.

Best for: Storm tracking, planning any outdoor work, wind assessment for renewable energy.

2. Weather Underground (Local Conditions)

What it does: Hyperlocal weather data from personal weather stations, integrated with historical records.

Why we use it: Weather Underground aggregates data from thousands of personal weather stations, giving neighborhood-level accuracy that national weather services cannot match. If you have a personal weather station, you can contribute your data and get full access.

Key features:

  • Station-specific forecasts: Enter your exact location (or weather station ID) for conditions from your neighborhood
  • Historical data: Search any date back years to see typical conditions
  • PWS network: Access to amateur weather station data worldwide
  • WunderMap: Interactive map showing real-time conditions from dozens of stations

Best for: Understanding local microclimates, planning garden work, hyperlocal temperature data.

3. NOAA National Weather Service (Reliability)

What it does: Official US weather forecasts, warnings, and long-range outlooks from the National Weather Service.

Why we use it: NOAA is the authoritative source for US weather. When it matters — tornado warnings, flood alerts, winter storms — NOAA is the source to trust. The interface is dated, but the data is reliable.

Key features:

  • Weather.gov: Enter your location for text forecasts, hourly/daily outlooks, and hazard information
  • WPC: Weather Prediction Center for national-scale maps and storm tracking
  • Storm Prediction Center: Outlooks for severe weather days in advance
  • National Hurricane Center: Tropical storm and hurricane tracking

The learning curve is real. Start at weather.gov with your specific location, then explore the linked resources.

Best for: Official warnings, winter storm planning, hurricane tracking, backup when other sources fail.

4. Clear Outside (Astronomy)

What it does: Cloud cover forecasts specifically optimized for stargazing and astrophotography.

Why we use it: Clear Outside is simple and niche — it forecasts cloud cover on an hourly basis, showing exactly when skies will clear. This is not a general weather site, but for anyone interested in astronomy or night sky watching, it is invaluable.

Key features:

  • Hourly cloud forecast: Shows cloud percentage hour-by-hour for up to a week
  • Moon phase: Integrated moon phase and rise/set times
  • Light pollution overlay: Shows artificial light levels from nearby cities
  • Best time suggestions: Highlights optimal observing windows

Best for: Planning stargazing, astrophotography, night photography.

5. Ventusky (Visualization)

What it does: Weather visualization with animated maps showing wind, precipitation, temperature, and pressure.

Why we use it: Ventusky is similar to Windy but with a different visual emphasis. The animations showing weather system movement are particularly useful for understanding how storms develop. It is slightly less feature-rich than Windy but more intuitive for beginners.

Key features:

  • Animated maps: Watch weather systems develop over hours
  • Multiple parameters: Temperature, precipitation, wind, pressure, humidity
  • Isoline display: Contour lines for easier pattern reading
  • Precipitation types: Distinguishes rain, snow, sleet

Best for: Visualizing weather patterns, understanding storm development, educational use.

6. AccuWeather (MinuteCast)

What it does: Minute-by-minute precipitation forecasts for the next 2 hours.

Why we use it: AccuWeather's MinuteCast is genuinely useful — it tells you exactly when precipitation will start and stop in the next 120 minutes. For planning outdoor work that cannot get wet, this is the most precise short-term forecast available.

Key features:

  • MinuteCast: Minute-by-minute precipitation forecast
  • Hourly planner: Visual hourly forecast for planning work windows
  • RealFeel: Accounts for wind and humidity in temperature perception
  • Long-range: 15, 25, and 45-day forecasts

Best for: Short-term activity planning, knowing exactly when to bring in firewood, timing outdoor tasks.

Secondary Resources

These are useful in specific situations:

7. National Hurricane Center (hurricanes.gov)

Specialized tracking for tropical systems. If you are anywhere near the Gulf Coast, Atlantic Coast, or Caribbean, this is your authoritative source for hurricane season information.

8. Snow Forecast (snow-forecast.com)

Ski-resort focused but useful for anyone wanting to track winter storms. Shows snowfall predictions for specific locations hours to days in advance.

9. Tide Forecast (tideforecast.com)

Simple, no-nonsense tide tables. Essential if you are coastal or doing any water-related activities.

10. UV Index (epa.gov/sunwise)

EPA's UV Index shows sun intensity for planning outdoor work. Useful for knowing when to wear sun protection.

Our Daily Weather Workflow

This is the sequence we actually use:

Morning check (10 minutes):

  1. Open Windy.com — check wind and cloud layers at our location
  2. Check AccuWeather MinuteCast — plan outdoor work windows for the next 4 hours
  3. Check NOAA weather.gov — review the 7-day forecast

Evening check (5 minutes):

  1. Open Windy.com — compare models for tomorrow
  2. Check AccuWeather — preview tomorrow's conditions

When storms approach (20 minutes):

  1. Open NOAA Storm Prediction Center — check outlook categories
  2. Open Ventusky — animate the approaching system
  3. Open Windy — track wind layers and precipitation

Key Differences We Found

We compared the most accurate forecasts across all sites. Here is what we learned:

ParameterBest SourceNotes
TemperatureNOAAMost consistent with actual
PrecipitationAccuWeatherMinuteCast is surprisingly accurate
WindWindyECMWF model is best
StormsNOAA SPCMost advance warning
Long-rangeWindyLonger horizon than others
Local microclimateWeather UndergroundPWS data is hyperlocal

Getting Started

Start with these three:

  1. Windy.com — Create a free account, save your location. Check it daily.
  2. AccuWeather — Install the app. Enable notifications for your location.
  3. NOAA weather.gov — Bookmark your local forecast page.

These three cover the majority of what you need. The others are supplements for specific situations.
