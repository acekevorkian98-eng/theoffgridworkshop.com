Composting with Earthworms: The Vermicomposting System That Works

We process 5 pounds of kitchen scraps per week into usable compost using earthworms. The system costs $75 to build, requires 15 minutes per week of maintenance, and produces roughly 2 pounds of finished compost per week from May through October.

By Jake Mercer · Off-Grid Homesteader & Former Engineer

In This Article

Why Vermicomposting Works

Vermicomposting uses red wiggle worms (Eisenia fetida, commonly called red wigglers) to break down organic matter. The worms consume food scraps and bedding, digest them, and produce castings — worm manure — that is one of the richest soil amendments available.

Compared to traditional composting:

AspectTraditional CompostVermicompost
Time to finished product3-6 months2-3 months
Space required4x4x3 ft minimum2x2x1 ft
TemperatureThermophilic (130-160°F)Ambient (55-85°F)
Turning requiredWeeklyMinimal
Input limitAny organic matterNo citrus, meat, dairy
Output qualityGoodExcellent

Vermicompost is not a replacement for traditional compost — it is a complement. We use both.

The Worms: Red Wigglers

Red wigglers (Eisenia fetida) are the standard composting worm. They are not the earthworms you find in your garden — they are a different species adapted to composting conditions.

Key characteristics:

  • Temperature range: 55-85°F (optimal: 65-75°F)
  • Lifespan: 1-2 years
  • Reproduction: Doubles population every 60-90 days under good conditions
  • Feeding rate: Half their body weight per day (roughly 1/4 lb per pound of worms)

Do not use garden earthworms. They die in composting conditions. Red wigglers are specifically adapted to this environment — they tolerate higher densities, different moisture levels, and can survive the bin conditions that kill common earthworms.

Our Worm Bin Design

We have tried four worm bin designs over three years. This is the one that works:

The Stackable Bin System

Materials:

  • Three 10-gallon plastic storage bins ($10 each)
  • One lid ($5)
  • Drill with 1/4 inch bit
  • Shredder paper (newspaper, cardboard)
  • Food scraps

Cost: $40-75 depending on what you have on hand.

Construction:

  1. Drill 1/4 inch ventilation holes in the sides of all three bins (8-10 holes per bin, spaced 2 inches apart)
  2. Drill 1/4 inch drainage holes in the bottom of the lowest bin (8-10 holes)
  3. Leave the lid loose (not sealed — worms need air)

That's it. No special construction required.

Why This Design Works

We tested single-bin systems, flow-through bins, and fancy worm towers. The stackable bin won because:

  1. Harvesting is easy: Once the bottom bin fills with castings, the worms migrate upward into fresh bedding above. You harvest the bottom bin without sorting worms.
  2. Temperature stability: The plastic provides insulation, and the volume holds heat better than shallow systems.
  3. NoSpecial tools: Everything is available at any hardware store.

The flow-through systems look good in videos but are harder to manage for beginners. Start with this simple design.

Setting Up Your Worm Bin

Step 1: Bedding

Fill the bin with 4-6 inches of moistened bedding. We use:

  • Newspaper (shredded): The primary bedding. Tear into 1-inch strips.
  • Cardboard (shredded): A great carbon source. Remove tape and staples.
  • Coconut coir (optional): Holds moisture well. Adds to the mix.

Bedding should feel like a wrung-out sponge — moist but not dripping. If you can squeeze water from it, it is too wet.

Step 2: The Worms

Start with roughly one pound of worms (roughly 1,000 worms). Where to get them:

  • Local bait shops: Red wigglers are sold as fishing bait. Call ahead — availability varies.
  • Online suppliers: $25-40 per pound, shipped. Vermicompost.com, Uncle Jim's Worm Farm.
  • Local friend: Worm bin owners often have extras to share.

Do not start with kitchen scraps immediately. Let the worms settle into their new bedding for 1-2 weeks before feeding.

Step 3: First Feeding

Start with a small amount — roughly 1 cup of scraps. Bury the food in the bedding rather than leaving it on top. Within a few days, the worms should be active and feeding.

Ongoing Care

Feeding Schedule

Feed 2-3 times per week. The general rule: feed only when the previous feeding is mostly consumed. Overfeeding causes problems (smell, flies, decomposition).

A well-populated bin (1+ pounds of worms) handles:

  • 3-5 cups of scraps per week
  • Roughly 5 pounds of kitchen waste from a family of 4

What to Feed

Red wigglers eat most kitchen scraps:

  • Vegetables: All vegetable scraps
  • Fruits: All fruit scraps (citrus in small amounts)
  • Coffee grounds: including paper filter
  • Tea bags: (without staples)
  • Bread and grains: In small amounts
  • Crushed eggshells: Added calcium

What NOT to Feed

These cause problems:

  • Citrus: In large quantities — acidity can harm worms. Small amounts are fine.
  • Onions and garlic: Alliums deter worms
  • Meat and dairy: Attracts pests, smellsbad, creates harmful bacteria
  • Oily foods: Creates anaerobic zones
  • Spicy foods: Can harm worm tissue
  • Pet waste: Contains pathogens

Moisture Management

The bin should feel like a wrung-out sponge. Too wet and it becomes anaerobic (smells, worms die). Too dry and the worms cannot move or feed.

Check moisture weekly. If it is drying out, mist with water. If too wet, add more dry bedding.

Temperature

Red wigglers thrive at 65-75°F. They slow significantly below 55°F and die above 85°F. In our zone 6b climate:

  • Summer: Bin goes to a shaded, cool location. Basement or under a deck works.
  • Winter: Bin comes inside or is heavily insulated. Ours goes into the garage — the temperature stays above freezing and the worms go dormant but survive.

If your bin is outdoors in summer, remember that plastic bins heat up significantly. A black bin in direct sun can hit 120°F. Shade is essential.

Harvesting the Castings

The castings (worm manure) are your finished product. They are dark, crumbly, and rich in nutrients.

The Simple Harvest Method

Our preferred method — works with the stackable design:

  1. Stop feeding for 2-3 weeks
  2. Place fresh bedding and a small amount of food in the top bin
  3. The worms migrate toward the food and light
  4. After 2-3 weeks, the bottom bin is mostly castings with few worms
  5. Remove the bottom bin — this is your finished compost
  6. Place the bottom bin back on top and repeat

This takes longer than sorting worms by hand, but it requires less handling of the worms.

Using Worm Castings

Worm castings are powerful. Use them:

  • As a top dressing: Spread 1/4 to 1/2 inch on garden beds.
  • In potting mix: Add 10-20% to potting soil.
  • As a tea: Steep 1 cup castings in 1 gallon water for 24 hours. Use to water plants.
  • In seedling mix: Add 10% to seed-starting mix.

Castings are gentle enough to use directly on seedlings without burning.

Troubleshooting

Problem: Bad Smell

Cause: Overfeeding, too wet, or meat/dairy in the bin.

Solution: Stop feeding for 1-2 weeks. Add dry bedding. If it smellsbad, empty partially and start fresh with new bedding.

Problem: Fruit Flies

Cause: Food left on top of bedding.

Solution: Always bury food scraps. If flies appear, place a layer of dry bedding on top and reduce feeding temporarily.

Problem: Worms Dying

Cause: Usually temperature (too hot or too cold) or moisture issues.

Solution: Check temperature first — 55-85°F is the range. Check moisture second — should be damp but not wet.

Problem: Bin Not Breaking Down

Cause: Too dry, too cold, or not enough worms.

Solution: Moisten the bedding. Ensure temperature is in range. Add more worms (the bin will balance naturally over time).

Our Updated Design

After three years, we have simplified further:

What we use now:

  • Two 18-gallon storage bins (one for active composting, one for finished material)
  • Worms live in one bin
  • When full, we move to the second bin with fresh bedding
  • The worms sort themselves in 2-3 weeks

This is simpler than the three-bin system and works equally well. Total cost: $25 for two bins.

Getting Started

You do not need much:

  1. Two plastic bins ($10-20): 10-18 gallon size
  2. Newspaper/Cardboard ($0): Save from recycling
  3. Red wigglers ($25-40): 1 pound
  4. Kitchen scraps ($0): What you already have

Total startup: $35-60. The system pays for itself in Year 1 in reduced trash disposal and garden amendment costs.
