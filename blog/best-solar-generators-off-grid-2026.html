What We Tested & How

Solar generators (also called portable power stations) are essential backup for off-gridders — they provide AC power when the main system goes down, run tools and appliances during cloudy stretches, and give you flexibility for seasonal or emergency loads. We tested six units side-by-side over a three-month period, measuring real watt-hour delivery, solar charge efficiency, AC surge capacity, and how much energy actually reached the battery after conversion losses.

Our test protocol:

  • Watt-hour delivery: Fully charged each unit and discharged through a calibrated load until the battery hit 5% (usable capacity). We recorded actual watt-hours delivered vs. rated capacity.
  • Solar charge time: Measured time to recharge from empty using 400W of solar input (typical for portable panels). Recorded charge rate at 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100% battery levels.
  • AC output surge capacity: Tested instantaneous power draw at startup of high-draw appliances (refrigerator compressor, induction cooktop, power tools). Verified the unit didn't shut down under load spikes.
  • Wall AC efficiency: Measured power required from wall outlet to reach full charge (accounting for inverter/charger losses).
  • Durability: Cycled each unit through 20 charge/discharge cycles, checked for thermal management issues, and inspected for any battery degradation.

All units tested use LiFePO4 (lithium iron phosphate) battery chemistry — the new standard across all three leading brands. LiFePO4 offers 3,000+ cycle life vs. 500–800 for older lithium-ion, making these units viable for long-term off-grid use.

Solar Panel Pairing: How Much Panel Capacity Do You Need?

A portable solar generator is only as good as its recharge capacity. Pair the generator with sufficient solar panel capacity to recharge within your cloudy-day window.

Example calculations:

  • Jackery 2000 Pro (2,160 Wh) with 400W MPPT: Takes 8 hours to recharge from 400W average solar. If you have 3 cloudy days, you need enough reserves to store 6,480 Wh (three days × 2,160 Wh) or use 600–800W of solar to recharge faster on partial-sun days.
  • EcoFlow Delta 2 Max (1,024 Wh) with 400W MPPT: Takes 7.5 hours. For 3 cloudy days, budget 3,072 Wh storage or pair with 600W solar for redundancy.
  • Budget rule-of-thumb: For every 1,000 Wh of battery capacity, plan 400–600W of solar panel input to recharge in 8–12 hours on a partly-cloudy day.

If your site gets consistent sun, 400W of panels per 2,000 Wh of battery is adequate. If cloud cover is frequent or seasonal, increase solar panel capacity to 800W+ or plan for oversized battery banks to cover longer cloudy stretches.

Why LiFePO4 Changes Everything

All six units we tested use LiFePO4 (lithium iron phosphate) battery chemistry, and this is the game-changer for off-gridders. Compared to older lithium-ion:

  • Cycle life: 3,000+ full charge/discharge cycles vs. 500–800 for Li-ion. This means 8–10 years of daily use vs. 2–3 years.
  • Thermal stability: LiFePO4 doesn't degrade as fast in high heat. Off-grid cabins with summer heat won't see battery degradation as quickly.
  • Safety: LiFePO4 is inherently more stable — less risk of thermal runaway or internal fires.
  • Cost per cycle: Higher upfront cost, but lower cost-per-use over the battery's lifetime.

This is why we recommend LiFePO4 units only — older Li-ion portable generators are being phased out and don't justify the cost difference.
