Gardening in Cold Weather: Our Year-Round Growing Strategy

We harvested lettuce on March 15 this year. We picked kale through December 28. In between, we grew peas in April, tomatoes through October, and spinach that survived under snow in January. Our zone 6b garden produces food from mid-March through late December — roughly 10 months of the year — without a heated greenhouse.

By Jake Mercer · Off-Grid Homesteader & Former Engineer

In This Article

Understanding Your Frost Window

Your growing season is defined by two dates: the last spring frost and the first fall frost. Everything else follows from knowing these boundaries.

We are in zone 6b (central Virginia), where the average last frost is April 25 and the first frost is October 15. That gives 173 frost-free days. But with cold frames, row covers, and careful crop selection, we extend that to roughly 300 days of harvestable growing time.

The key insight is that many crops grow well below their optimal temperature — they just grow slower. A tomato at 55°F air temperature barely grows. But lettuce at 45°F puts on measurable growth. Spinach at 35°F slows but does not stop. The crops that survive winter are not tropical plants forced into cold conditions — they are cold-tolerant species grown in their preferred temperature range.

The Crops That Work in Cold Weather

Not all vegetables tolerate cold. We focus on crops that prefer cool conditions:

Cold-Hardy Crops (Survive Light Frost)

These crops tolerate temperatures down to 28°F and often lower with protection:

  • Spinach: The hardiest green. We have harvested spinach under 4 inches of snow. It grows best between 40-60°F and survives dips to 20°F. Direct sow in fall for spring harvest, or in late winter for earliest spring greens.
  • Kale: Survives to 20°F and gets sweeter after frost. The cold converts starches to sugars, making late-fall kale notably sweeter than summer-harvested. We grow 'Lacinato' for cooking and 'Red Russian' for fresh salads.
  • Collards: Similar to kale but harder. We have kept collards alive through zone 6 winters with just row cover protection. They are the most reliable winter green.
  • Lettuce: Most varieties tolerate down to 28°F. Butterhead and romaine are more cold-tolerant than iceberg types. Direct sow every 2 weeks from early spring through fall for continuous harvest.
  • Arugula: Bolts quickly in heat — but grows well in cool conditions. Plant in spring and fall. We harvest arugula from March through June, then again from September through November.
  • Mustard Greens: Fast-growing and cold-tolerant. 'Southern Giant' is particularly hardy. Ready in 40 days from direct sow.
  • Peas: Direct sow as soon as soil reaches 45°F. Peas require cool weather — they stop producing once temperatures exceed 75°F. Our spring pea window is April 1 through June 15.

Cool-Season Crops (Prefer 50-65°F)

These crops do not tolerate frost but grow in cool spring and fall conditions:

  • Carrots: Direct sow in early spring. Carrots take 70-90 days and taste sweeter after frost. We sow every 3 weeks from March through August for continuous harvest.
  • Beets: Both roots and greens are edible. Beets tolerate light frost and grow best between 50-65°F. Direct sow every 3 weeks from April through August.
  • Radishes: Ready in 25-30 days. The fastest crop in our garden. We sow every week from early spring through fall. Multiple Succession Plantings are the key to continuous harvest.
  • Swiss Chard: Grows continuously from spring through fall. Some varieties overwinter with protection. The colorful stems make it attractive enough to plant in ornamental beds.
  • Broccoli: Start indoors 6 weeks before last frost, transplant after soil reaches 50°F. Broccoli prefers 60-70°F — too warm in summer, but perfect in spring and fall.
  • Cauliflower: Similarly started indoors. Requires consistent moisture — drought causes buttoning (premature heading).

Warm-Season Crops (Needs 65°F+ soil)

These cannot be rushed — soil temperature matters more than air temperature:

  • Tomatoes: Wait until soil reaches 60°F. We use black plastic mulch to warm soil 2 weeks earlier. Plant after last frost date.
  • Peppers: Even more heat-loving. Start indoors 8-10 weeks before last frost. Transplate after soil reaches 65°F.
  • Beans: Direct sow after last frost when soil reaches 60°F. Beans fix their own nitrogen — plant where heavy feeders grew the previous year.
  • Squash: Direct sow after last frost. Hills should be 4-6 feet apart for vining varieties.

Season Extension Tools

We use four levels of protection, escalating based on how late or early we want to grow:

Level 1: Row Cover (Light Protection)

Row cover is a spun-bound polyester fabric that lets light and water through but provides 4-8°F of frost protection. Drape over plants and weight down the edges with soil, rocks, or sandbags.

We use Agribon AG-19 (1.0 oz/sq ft) for most applications. It provides up to 4°F of protection and lasts 3-4 seasons with careful handling.

Cost: $0.08-0.12 per square foot. A 10x100 foot roll costs roughly $40 and covers 1,000 square feet.

Application: Drape over transplants immediately after planting. Remove for harvesting and replace after. The fabric is light enough not to crush plants, but it does reduce light transmission by roughly 10%.

Level 2: Cold Frame (Medium Protection)

A cold frame is a transparent box that traps solar heat. We covered this in detail in our Cold Frames guide, but for this article, here is the key information:

  • A well-designed cold frame reaches 75-85°F on a sunny March day when outdoor temperature is 45°F
  • At night, an insulated cold frame stays 15-20°F warmer than outside air
  • This extends your season by 6-8 weeks in both spring and fall

We built our primary cold frame from cedar 2x6 lumber with a hinged polycarbonate lid. Total cost: $125. It has paid for itself in three seasons of extended harvest.

Level 3: Low Tunnel (Row-Scale Protection)

A low tunnel is a row cover stretched over wire hoops. You can cover an entire garden row — 50-100 feet of crops — with one tunnel.

The standard approach:

  1. Drive 1/2-inch EMT conduit into the ground every 6 feet along your row
  2. Bend the conduit into a hoop (3-4 feet wide, 2-3 feet tall)
  3. Drape row cover over the hoops
  4. Secure edges with soil or ground stakes

A 50-foot low tunnel with row cover costs roughly $50. The conduit and ground stakes are reusable for many seasons.

Temperature performance: On sunny days, a low tunnel reaches 15-25°F warmer than outside. At night, it provides 4-8°F of frost protection.

Level 4: High Tunnel / Polytunnel (Maximum Protection)

A high tunnel is a walk-in greenhouse structure without foundation. Unlike a permanent greenhouse, a high tunnel is typically unheated, manually vented, and anchoreddirectly into the ground.

We have not built a high tunnel yet — the cost is $1-4 per square foot, and our cold frames handle our needs. But for serious year-round production, this is the next step.

Commercial high tunnel kits start at $500 for a 10x20 foot structure. DIY builds using PVC pipe and poly film can be cheaper but have shorter lifespans.

Our Year-Round Planting Calendar

This is our actual planting schedule based on our zone 6b location:

February-March (Early Spring)

  • February 15: Start spinach and lettuce indoors under lights. These go into cold frames by March 1.
  • March 1: Direct sow arugula, mustard greens, and spinach in cold frame.
  • March 15: Transplant lettuce from indoors to cold frame. First harvest from cold frame begins.
  • March 15-April 1: Direct sow peas in garden beds.

April (Main Spring Plant)

  • April 1: Transplant broccoli and cauliflower seedlings to garden.
  • April 10: Direct sow carrots, beets, radishes, Swiss chard.
  • April 15: Start tomatoes and peppers indoors.
  • April 25: Last frost date. Transplant first tomatoes under wall-o-water or similar protection.
  • April 25-May 1: Transplant lettuce and spinach to garden (row cover ready).

May-June (Warm-Season Plant)

  • May 1: Direct sow beans after last frost.
  • May 15: Transplant tomatoes, peppers, squash (all warm-season crops).
  • May 20: Harvest spring lettuce, arugula, spinach. Let remaining bolt for seed.
  • June 1: Second planting of beans, carrots, beets for succession.

July-August (Summer Maintenance)

  • July 1: Start fall crops indoors — broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage.
  • July 15: Stop harvesting spring-planted crops. Pull spent plants.
  • July 15-August 15: Direct sow fall lettuce, arugula, spinach in shaded areas.
  • August 1: Transplant fall brassicas to garden.

September-October (Fall Harvest)

  • September 1: Direct sow fall carrots, beets, radishes.
  • September 15: Harvest tomatoes, peppers. Install cold frames over remaining crops.
  • October 1: Harvest beans, squash. Pull warm-season plants.
  • October 15: First frost date. Harvest remaining warm crops. Cold frame crops continue.
  • October 15-November 1: Direct sow overwintering spinach and garlic.

November-December (Winter Harvest)

  • November 1: Install low tunnels over remaining beds.
  • November 15: Harvest remaining greens. Close cold frames for winter.
  • December 1: Last outdoor harvest — kale, collards, spinach.
  • December-January: Cold frame harvest continues in mild weather.

Common Mistakes We Made

Three errors cost us the most time:

1. Planting too early. We transplanted tomatoes before soil reached 60°F. They sat dormant for three weeks, then caught up once warmed. Not worth the stress. Wait for soil temperature.

2. Ignoring succession planting. A single planting of lettuce bolts all at once. Multiple small plantings every 2-3 weeks beats one large planting. This is the single most impactful practice.

3. Underwatering in winter. We assumed cold frames did not need water in winter. They do — especially on sunny winter days when the sun evaporates moisture inside the closed frame. Check soil moisture weekly even in winter.

Getting Started

You do not need every tool at once. Start with row cover and one cold-hardy crop (spinach or lettuce), then expand from there.

Your minimum viable cold-weather garden:

  1. Row cover ($40): Covers 1,000 sq ft, provides 4-8°F protection
  2. Spinach seeds ($3): Direct sow, cold-hardy
  3. Lettuce seeds ($3): Direct sow, cold-tolerant

Total startup cost: $50. First harvest in as little as 45 days.

Expand next year with a cold frame and a second bed of cold-hardy crops. Within three years, you will have extended your harvest season by 4-6 months with minimal investment.
