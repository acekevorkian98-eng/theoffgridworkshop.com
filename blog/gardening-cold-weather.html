Gardening in Cold Weather: Our Year-Round Growing Strategy

We harvested lettuce on March 15 this year. We picked kale through December 28. In between, we grew peas in April, tomatoes through October, and spinach that survived under snow in January. Our zone 6b garden produces food from mid-March through late December — roughly 10 months of the year — without a heated greenhouse.

By Jake Mercer · Off-Grid Homesteader & Former Engineer

In This Article

Understanding Your Frost Window

Your growing season is defined by two dates: the last spring frost and the first fall frost. Everything else follows from knowing these boundaries.

We are in zone 6b (central Virginia), where the average last frost is April 25 and the first frost is October 15. That gives 173 frost-free days. But with cold frames, row covers, and careful crop selection, we extend that to roughly 300 days of harvestable growing time.

The key insight is that many crops grow well below their optimal temperature — they just grow slower. A tomato at 55°F air temperature barely grows. But lettuce at 45°F puts on measurable growth. Spinach at 35°F slows but does not stop. The crops that survive winter are not tropical plants forced into cold conditions — they are cold-tolerant species grown in their preferred temperature range.

But here is what took us three years to learn: frost dates are not a wall. They are a probability curve. In 2024, our last frost was April 10 — two weeks early. In 2025, it was May 8 — two weeks late. The single most valuable investment we made was tracking our own frost dates for five consecutive years. We now know that our actual window is April 15 +/- 3 weeks.

Where to find your actual frost dates:

  • NOAAs Climate Data Center — historical frost data by state and county
  • Your state extension service — often has precise local data
  • Your neighbors — ask three people who garden. Take the earliest and latest dates they remember
  • Your own records — start tracking this year. Keep a simple notebook or spreadsheet

For the first two years, we over-planted by assuming the early frost date would be late (planting too early) and the late frost date would be early (planting too late). The middle path — planting for the average date, but covering everything with row cover for the first two weeks — is what works.

The Crops That Work in Cold Weather

Not all vegetables tolerate cold. We focus on crops that prefer cool conditions:

The Physiology of Cold-Tolerant Plants

Before we list specific crops, here is the key principle that will guide every decision you make: cold-tolerant plants have anti-freeze proteins in their cell walls. When temperatures drop below 32°F, these proteins prevent ice crystals from forming inside the cells — they do not stop ice from forming between cells, which is why the plant can look frozen but still recover.

Cold-tolerant plants include every crop in the brassica family (kale, collards, broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts), every leafy green we eat (spinach, lettuce, chard, arugula), and root vegetables (carrots, beets, radishes, turnips). Every crop in this category can survive at least a light frost (28-32°F). The ones that fail are the warm-season crops — tomatoes, peppers, beans, squash — whose cell walls rupture when ice forms inside.

This physiological distinction is why we separate crops into three categories: cold-hardy (survive below 28°F), cool-season (survive light frost but prefer 50-65°F), and warm-season (die at frost, need 65°F+ soil). Once you understand this, planning becomes easy.

Cold-Hardy Crops (Survive Light Frost)

These crops tolerate temperatures down to 28°F and often lower with protection:

  • Spinach: The hardiest green. We have harvested spinach under 4 inches of snow. It grows best between 40-60°F and survives dips to 20°F. Direct sow in fall for spring harvest, or in late winter for earliest spring greens. The variety matters: 'Bloomsdale Long Standing' is our go-to for spring planting; 'Winter Blocker' is what we sow in fall for overwintering.
  • Kale: Survives to 20°F and gets sweeter after frost. The cold converts starches to sugars, making late-fall kale notably sweeter than summer-harvested. We grow 'Lacinato' for cooking and 'Red Russian' for fresh salads. Planted in August, we harvest Lacinato from October through December, then again in March after it winters over.
  • Collards: Similar to kale but harder. We have kept collards alive through zone 6 winters with just row cover protection. They are the most reliable winter green. Unlike kale, collards do not get bitter in cold. 'Georgia' is our standard variety.
  • Lettuce: Most varieties tolerate down to 28°F. Butterhead and romaine are more cold-tolerant than iceberg types. Direct sow every 2 weeks from early spring through fall for continuous harvest. The key insight: lettuce goes dormant above 80°F, not just below 32°F. Plant it in spring and fall; let it rest in summer.
  • Arugula: Bolts quickly in heat — but grows well in cool conditions. Plant in spring and fall. We harvest arugula from March through June, then again from September through November. The wild arugula (Diplotaxis tenuifolia) is perennial and less prone to bolting than the annual variety.
  • Mustard Greens: Fast-growing and cold-tolerant. 'Southern Giant' is particularly hardy. Ready in 40 days from direct sow. The purple-veined varieties are both ornamental and edible — we plant them in the flower beds alongside our herbs.
  • Peas: Direct sow as soon as soil reaches 45°F. Peas require cool weather — they stop producing once temperatures exceed 75°F. Our spring pea window is April 1 through June 15. Sugar snap peas produce longer than shell peas; we plant both.
  • Cabbage and Brussels Sprouts: Both tolerate light frost and actually need cold to develop their flavor. Brussels sprouts are the last thing we harvest in winter — we leave them on the stalk through December because a light frost makes them noticeably sweeter.
  • Turnips and Rutabagas: Both roots and greens are edible. Turnips are ready in 40-50 days; rutabagas take 80-90 days but store better. 'Purple Top' is our standard turnip; 'Joan' is a good rutabaga variety.

Cool-Season Crops (Prefer 50-65°F)

These crops do not tolerate frost but grow in cool spring and fall conditions:

  • Carrots: Direct sow in early spring. Carrots take 70-90 days and taste sweeter after frost. We sow every 3 weeks from March through August for continuous harvest. The variety matters: 'Nantes' is our go-to for spring (it is cold-tolerant); 'Danvers' is better for storage (we plant it in August for fall harvest). Direct sow only — carrots transplant poorly.
  • Beets: Both roots and greens are edible. Beets tolerate light frost and grow best between 50-65°F. Direct sow every 3 weeks from April through August. 'Chioggia' is our favorite for the concentric rings; 'Detroit Dark Red' is the standard for canning.
  • Radishes: Ready in 25-30 days. The fastest crop in our garden. We sow every week from early spring through fall. Multiple Succession Plantings are the key to continuous harvest. The winter radish ('Black Spanish') takes 60 days but stores for months.
  • Swiss Chard: Grows continuously from spring through fall. Some varieties overwinter with protection. The colorful stems make it attractive enough to plant in ornamental beds. 'Bright Lights' is our mix; 'Fordhook Giant' is the standard white stem.
  • Broccoli: Start indoors 6 weeks before last frost, transplant after soil reaches 50°F. Broccoli prefers 60-70°F — too warm in summer, but perfect in spring and fall. We start a second crop in July for fall harvest.
  • Cauliflower: Similarly started indoors. Requires consistent moisture — drought causes buttoning (premature heading). The Romanesco varieties (spiral head) are more cold-tolerant than standard white cauliflower.
  • Kohlrabi: Often overlooked but one of our best producers. Ready in 45-55 days, tolerance for both cold and heat, and the flavor is milder than broccoli. 'Early White Vienna' is our standard.

Our rule of thumb for cool-season crops: if the daytime high has been above 75°F for more than a week, stop planting. The plants will struggle all summer. Wait until August to plant again for fall harvest.

Warm-Season Crops (Needs 65°F+ soil)

These cannot be rushed — soil temperature matters more than air temperature:

  • Tomatoes: Wait until soil reaches 60°F. We use black plastic mulch to warm soil 2 weeks earlier. Plant after last frost date.
  • Peppers: Even more heat-loving. Start indoors 8-10 weeks before last frost. Transplate after soil reaches 65°F.
  • Beans: Direct sow after last frost when soil reaches 60°F. Beans fix their own nitrogen — plant where heavy feeders grew the previous year.
  • Squash: Direct sow after last frost. Hills should be 4-6 feet apart for vining varieties.

Season Extension Tools

We use four levels of protection, escalating based on how late or early we want to grow:

Level 1: Row Cover (Light Protection)

Row cover is a spun-bound polyester fabric that lets light and water through but provides 4-8°F of frost protection. Drape over plants and weight down the edges with soil, rocks, or sandbags.

We use Agribon AG-19 (1.0 oz/sq ft) for most applications. It provides up to 4°F of protection and lasts 3-4 seasons with careful handling.

Cost: $0.08-0.12 per square foot. A 10x100 foot roll costs roughly $40 and covers 1,000 square feet.

Application: Drape over transplants immediately after planting. Remove for harvesting and replace after. The fabric is light enough not to crush plants, but it does reduce light transmission by roughly 10%.

Pro tip: Support the row cover with wire hoops (1/2-inch PVC pipe or EMT conduit bent into arches) rather than draping directly on plants. Direct contact conducts cold through the fabric to the leaves beneath. Hoops create an air gap that adds 2-4°F of additional protection.

The weight of the fabric matters: lighter weights (0.5 oz/sq ft) provide less protection but let in more light; heavier weights (1.5 oz/sq ft) provide more protection but reduce light significantly. We use 0.5 oz in early spring when light is limited, and 1.0 oz in late fall when light is abundant.

Level 2: Cold Frame (Medium Protection)

A cold frame is a transparent box that traps solar heat. We covered this in detail in our Cold Frames guide, but for this article, here is the key information:

  • A well-designed cold frame reaches 75-85°F on a sunny March day when outdoor temperature is 45°F
  • At night, an insulated cold frame stays 15-20°F warmer than outside air
  • This extends your season by 6-8 weeks in both spring and fall

We built our primary cold frame from cedar 2x6 lumber with a hinged polycarbonate lid. Total cost: $125. It has paid for itself in three seasons of extended harvest.

Two critical details we learned the hard way: first, vent on sunny days even in winter. On a 45°F day, an unvented cold frame hits 90°F. That shocks the plants worse than the cold. Leave the lid propped open 2-3 inches whenever the sun is up. Second, water in winter. We assumed closed cold frames stay moist; they dry out faster than we expected because the sun evaporates water even in December.

The placement matters: orient the cold frame to face south (or the direction of your most direct winter sun). Against a south-facing wall adds 5-10°F of reflected heat. We built ours against the garage foundation.

Level 3: Low Tunnel (Row-Scale Protection)

A low tunnel is a row cover stretched over wire hoops. You can cover an entire garden row — 50-100 feet of crops — with one tunnel.

The standard approach:

  1. Drive 1/2-inch EMT conduit into the ground every 6 feet along your row
  2. Bend the conduit into a hoop (3-4 feet wide, 2-3 feet tall)
  3. Drape row cover over the hoops
  4. Secure edges with soil or ground stakes

A 50-foot low tunnel with row cover costs roughly $50. The conduit and ground stakes are reusable for many seasons.

Temperature performance: On sunny days, a low tunnel reaches 15-25°F warmer than outside. At night, it provides 4-8°F of frost protection.

The width matters more than you expect. A 4-foot-wide tunnel has less volume relative to its surface area than a 6-foot-wide one — it heats faster and cools faster. We use 4-foot-wide for early spring (quick warming) and 6-foot-wide for fall (slower temperature changes). The height should match your tallest crop — 2-3 feet for most greens and brassicas.

Ventilation is essential: roll up the sides on sunny days above 50°F. We use a simple pulley system with hooks on the ends to hold the rolled fabric. It takes 30 seconds to vent or close.

Level 4: High Tunnel / Polytunnel (Maximum Protection)

A high tunnel is a walk-in greenhouse structure without foundation. Unlike a permanent greenhouse, a high tunnel is typically unheated, manually vented, and anchoreddirectly into the ground.

We have not built a high tunnel yet — the cost is $1-4 per square foot, and our cold frames handle our needs. But for serious year-round production, this is the next step.

Commercial high tunnel kits start at $500 for a 10x20 foot structure. DIY builds using PVC pipe and poly film can be cheaper but have shorter lifespans.

Our Year-Round Planting Calendar

This is our actual planting schedule based on our zone 6b location:

February-March (Early Spring)

  • February 15: Start spinach and lettuce indoors under lights. These go into cold frames by March 1.
  • March 1: Direct sow arugula, mustard greens, and spinach in cold frame.
  • March 15: Transplant lettuce from indoors to cold frame. First harvest from cold frame begins.
  • March 15-April 1: Direct sow peas in garden beds.

April (Main Spring Plant)

  • April 1: Transplant broccoli and cauliflower seedlings to garden.
  • April 10: Direct sow carrots, beets, radishes, Swiss chard.
  • April 15: Start tomatoes and peppers indoors.
  • April 25: Last frost date. Transplant first tomatoes under wall-o-water or similar protection.
  • April 25-May 1: Transplant lettuce and spinach to garden (row cover ready).

May-June (Warm-Season Plant)

  • May 1: Direct sow beans after last frost.
  • May 15: Transplant tomatoes, peppers, squash (all warm-season crops).
  • May 20: Harvest spring lettuce, arugula, spinach. Let remaining bolt for seed.
  • June 1: Second planting of beans, carrots, beets for succession.

July-August (Summer Maintenance)

  • July 1: Start fall crops indoors — broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage.
  • July 15: Stop harvesting spring-planted crops. Pull spent plants.
  • July 15-August 15: Direct sow fall lettuce, arugula, spinach in shaded areas.
  • August 1: Transplant fall brassicas to garden.

September-October (Fall Harvest)

  • September 1: Direct sow fall carrots, beets, radishes.
  • September 15: Harvest tomatoes, peppers. Install cold frames over remaining crops.
  • October 1: Harvest beans, squash. Pull warm-season plants.
  • October 15: First frost date. Harvest remaining warm crops. Cold frame crops continue.
  • October 15-November 1: Direct sow overwintering spinach and garlic.

November-December (Winter Harvest)

  • November 1: Install low tunnels over remaining beds.
  • November 15: Harvest remaining greens. Close cold frames for winter.
  • December 1: Last outdoor harvest — kale, collards, spinach.
  • December-January: Cold frame harvest continues in mild weather.

Common Mistakes We Made

Three errors cost us the most time:

1. Planting too early. We transplanted tomatoes before soil reached 60°F. They sat dormant for three weeks, then caught up once warmed. Not worth the stress. Wait for soil temperature. The soil thermometer is $8 and saved us more than any other tool.

2. Ignoring succession planting. A single planting of lettuce bolts all at once. Multiple small plantings every 2-3 weeks beats one large planting. This is the single most impactful practice. We now have a succession planting calendar pinned to the wall: a row for each crop, with dates marked. Every Sunday, we check the calendar and plant whatever is due.

3. Underwatering in winter. We assumed cold frames did not need water in winter. They do — especially on sunny winter days when the sun evaporates moisture inside the closed frame. Check soil moisture weekly even in winter. We stick a finger in the soil every time we walk by. If it is dry to the first knuckle, water.

4. Not hardening off transplants. We started seedlings under lights indoors, then moved them directly to cold frames. They shocked for two weeks. Now we spend 7-10 days transitioning them: first to a shaded outdoor spot, then to the cold frame with the lid open, then to permanent placement.

5. Neglecting soil temperature. We obsessed over air temperature but ignored soil temperature. A 50°F air day can hide a 40°F soil temp. The soil warms slower than the air and cools slower too. A soil thermometer is mandatory for spring planting decisions.

Getting Started

You do not need every tool at once. Start with row cover and one cold-hardy crop (spinach or lettuce), then expand from there.

Your minimum viable cold-weather garden:

  1. Row cover ($40): Covers 1,000 sq ft, provides 4-8°F protection
  2. Spinach seeds ($3): Direct sow, cold-hardy
  3. Lettuce seeds ($3): Direct sow, cold-tolerant

Total startup cost: $50. First harvest in as little as 45 days.

Expand next year with a cold frame and a second bed of cold-hardy crops. Within three years, you will have extended your harvest season by 4-6 months with minimal investment.
