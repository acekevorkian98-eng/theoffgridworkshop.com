Building a Cob Oven for Baking Bread Off-Grid

A cob oven bakes better bread than any electric oven, costs under $50 in materials, and lasts decades. Here is the complete build with real temperature data from 40+ firings — including the failures that taught us the most.

By Jake Mercer · Off-Grid Homesteader & Former Engineer

In This Article

What We Learned From 40+ Firings

We have been firing our cob oven for 18 months. In that time, we have burned through roughly 2 cords of mixed hardwood, baked 60+ loaves of bread, roasted dozens of chickens, and made more mistakes than we can count. This article is the build guide we wish we had had — not the theory, but what actually works after nearly two years of real use.

We are not the first to build a cob oven. But we may be the first to document what actually happens after 40+ firings: the temperature curves, the wood consumption, the failures, and the surprises that no tutorial mentions.

Why a Cob Oven

Wood-fired ovens reach 700-900°F — temperatures that turn bread into something genuinely different from anything you can make in a domestic electric oven. The thermal mass of the cob dome stores heat and releases it evenly over hours, giving you descending-temperature baking that is ideal for bread (high heat initial burst for oven spring, then dropping temperature for a long, even bake).

For off-grid living, the practical case is straightforward:

  • No electricity required. Fire from your woodpile runs the oven.
  • Minimal materials. Clay, sand, and straw are either on your land or available locally.
  • Multi-use. The same fire bakes bread, roasts chicken, cooks beans overnight, and dries herbs.

Our oven cost $47 in materials. The timber for the stand was leftover from another project. If you have to buy everything, budget $80-120. Either way, it is a fraction of a commercial bread oven.

How a Cob Oven Works

The principle is thermal mass:

  1. Build a fire inside the dome for 1.5-3 hours
  2. Heat the cob walls to 600°F+
  3. Rake the coals out (or push to the side for retained-heat cooking)
  4. Slide the bread in

The dome radiates stored heat evenly from all sides — top and bottom simultaneously — with no hot spots or cold spots. This is the key difference from a conventional oven: radiant heat from clay acts like stone, cooking from every direction at once.

The Door Ratio Matters

The door height must be 63% of the interior dome height. Too tall and the heat escapes. Too short and the fire smolders from poor draft. Measure twice, cut once — this is the one calculation that actually matters.

Building the Foundation and Stand

The oven needs to be at a comfortable working height — waist-height (32-36 inches). We built a stand from salvaged cinderblocks on a level, stable base.

Step 1: Choose a spot 40+ feet from any structures. Open air matters for fire safety.

Step 2: Set cinderblocks on gravel. Fill the interior with rubble for thermal mass that holds heat.

Step 3: Top with 4 inches of sand as an insulating pad.

Step 4: Lay fire bricks flat on the sand, tightly fitted, no gaps.

The fire brick floor takes the most abuse. A 24-inch diameter gives room for two large loaves. Ours is 26 inches — room for three loaves.

The Cob Mix: What Actually Works

Cob is clay + sand + straw. The ratio matters more than any tutorial says:

  • Inner thermal layer: 1 part clay : 1 part sand (no straw). Straw burns out at high temperature and weakens the inner layer.
  • Outer insulating layer: 1 part clay : 1 part sand : 2 parts straw by volume.

Test your clay: Grab a fist-sized ball and let it dry. If it shrinks massively and cracks, it is too much clay — add sand. If it crumbles when dry, not enough clay. A workable mix holds its shape when squeezed and does not stick aggressively to your hands.

Our mix was 1:1 clay:sand for the inner layer, 1:1:1.5 for the outer. The extra sand reduced cracking significantly.

Building the Dome: Step by Step

  1. Make the sand form: Pile damp sand on the fire brick floor into a dome. Pack firmly. Inside diameter: 24-26 inches. Height: 16-20 inches. Cover with a single layer of damp newspaper — this tells you where the sand form ends when you excavate.
  2. Apply inner thermal layer: Pack 3-4 inches of clay-sand cob (no straw) firmly over the entire sand dome. Work from base up in horizontal bands. Press hard to eliminate voids. Let this layer stiffen for several hours before continuing.
  3. Cut the door opening: When the inner layer is leather-hard, mark and cut the door. Width: 8-10 inches. Height = 63% of interior dome height. For a 16-inch interior height, the opening is 10 inches tall.
  4. Excavate the sand form: Scoop out the sand through the door opening. The newspaper layer shows when you have reached the inner cob surface. Save the sand — you can reuse it.
  5. Apply outer insulating layer: 3-4 inches of clay-sand-straw cob over the dome. This layer retains heat during baking.
  6. Apply finish coat: A thin layer of pure straw-clay slip (very wet clay mix) as a weather-resistant outer skin.
  7. Dry thoroughly: Allow 2-3 weeks of air drying before the first fire. We made the mistake of firing too early — hairline cracks everywhere.

Curing: The Critical First Three Weeks

After air drying, the oven still holds moisture deep in the cob walls. Curing fires drive that moisture out slowly:

3-445 minTouch warm on exterior

15-212+ hoursReady for baking

Do not rush this. We rushed it and paid with cracks. Curing fires should feel warm, not hot. If you see steam, you are at the right temperature. Steam means moisture leaving the cob — that is the goal.

Hairline Cracks Are Normal

Some cracking is expected during curing. It is the clay contracting as it dries. Fill hairline cracks with clay-sand slip (the consistency of heavy cream). Press firmly into the crack and smooth flush. Cracks wider than 1/4 inch indicate a mix problem — too much clay.

Key finding: 2 hours is our sweet spot. We get bread-grade heat (450°F+ at the baking surface) with 12 pounds of wood. Adding more wood after 2.5 hours provides diminishing returns — the oven is already saturated with heat.

Temperature drop over baking: After we pull the bread at 45 minutes, the dome is still at 350°F. That's enough to roast vegetables, then herbs to dry. One firing, three hours of cooking.

Our Firing Log: What We Actually Use

After 18 months, here is the procedure that works:

For Bread (2-3 loaves)

  1. Build a small fire in the center (kindling + 3-4 chunks)
  2. After 15 minutes, add 6-8 more pieces around the edges
  3. Keep the fire burning strongly for 2 hours
  4. At 1.5 hours, rake most coals to one side
  5. At 2 hours, sweep the floor clean
  6. Check temp: if it sizzles a drop of water immediately, ~500°F
  7. Slide loaves in, seal the door
  8. Bake 35-45 minutes

Wood note: We use mixed hardwood (oak, maple, hickory). Pine works but imparts flavor and burns faster. Wet wood is the enemy — it produces creosote and wastes heat. Stack wood under cover for 6+ months before burning.

For Pizza

  1. Fire for 2.5 hours with the hottest fire you can manage
  2. Push all coals to one side
  3. Pizza at high heat cooks in 90-120 seconds
  4. Rotate halfway with a peel

Pizza temp: We have achieved 700°F+ at the baking surface. That is restaurant-grade.

Retained Heat Cooking

After bread baking, the oven drops through a long, useful gradient:

  • 350-400°F: Roast vegetables
  • 300°F: Covered cast iron pot of beans
  • 200°F: Herbs to dry on the floor

One firing, four hours of cooking, no additional fuel. This is where the cob oven shines.

The Failures That Taught Us

We have made every mistake. Here is what not to do:

  • Fired too soon. Cracked everywhere. Waited 2 weeks instead of 2-3 weeks.
  • Wrong clay ratio. First batch was too much clay. Shrink cracks 1 inch deep. Added sand to subsequent batches.
  • Forgot the door ratio. First door was too tall. Heat poured out. Reduced height by 2 inches.
  • No rain protection. Rain on a warm oven = thermal shock crack. Built a simple roof.
  • Wet wood. 50% less heat output. Now we dry wood for 6+ months.

Weather Protection: The Simple Fix

An outdoor cob oven needs a roof. This is not optional.

Rain on a warm oven causes thermal shock cracking. Rain on a cold oven dissolves the outer clay layer, season by season. Our simple 4-post shelter with a corrugated metal roof was $35 and added negligible maintenance.

If a shelter is not feasible, apply a limewash or earthen plaster finish to the exterior each spring. Inspect annually for cracks wide enough to admit water.

What We Would Build Differently

  • Wider door. Our 8-inch door is tight for a pizza peel. 10 inches would fit a standard peel.
  • Bigger oven. 26 inches is enough for 2 loaves. 32 inches would fit a baking stone for pizza.
  • Thermocouple port. We drill a temporary hole for the laser thermometer. A built-in port would be easier.
  • Firebrick floor. The floor has held up but shows wear. Would use harder firebrick next time.
