Chicken Coop Construction

📅 Published October 22, 2025 · ⏱️ 12 min read

Building a chicken coop is one of the most rewarding homestead projects. Fresh eggs daily, natural pest control, and excellent fertilizer make chickens incredibly valuable. But poor coop design leads to predator losses, disease, and unhappy birds. We've built three coops over eight years—here's everything we learned the hard way so you don't have to.

Planning Your Coop

Size Requirements

The golden rule: 4 square feet per bird inside, 10 square feet per bird in the run.

For 6 chickens:

  • Coop interior: 24 square feet minimum (4×6 or 3×8)
  • Outdoor run: 60 square feet minimum (6×10)

Critical: These are minimums. More space = healthier, happier chickens with less pecking/aggression. We built 6 sq ft per bird and they're noticeably calmer.

Location Selection

Choose your site carefully—moving a coop later is miserable:

  • Well-drained ground: Avoid low spots that collect water
  • Morning sun: Chickens appreciate warm morning light
  • Afternoon shade: Prevents overheating in summer
  • Wind protection: Shield from prevailing winter winds
  • Close to house: Makes daily care easier
  • Level ground: Or be prepared to level it yourself

Essential Coop Features

1. Roosting Bars

Chickens sleep on roosts, not in nesting boxes. Requirements:

  • Height: 2-4 feet off the ground
  • Space: 10-12 inches per chicken
  • Width: 2×3 or 2×4 lumber works well
  • Orientation: Flat, not on edge (chickens don't perch like parrots)
  • Spacing: If multiple bars, stagger 12" apart vertically

We use 2×3s placed with the wide side up—chickens settle their bodies over their feet for warmth.

2. Nesting Boxes

One box per 3-4 hens, though they'll all want the same one anyway!

Dimensions:

  • 12" wide × 12" deep × 12" tall for standard breeds
  • 14" × 14" × 14" for larger breeds

Features:

  • Mounted 18-24" off floor (lower than roosts)
  • Filled with soft bedding (pine shavings, straw)
  • Sloped roof prevents roosting on top (and pooping in boxes)
  • External access door makes egg collection easy

3. Ventilation

Probably the most overlooked aspect! Chickens produce surprising amounts of moisture and ammonia.

  • High vents: Place near roof peak for heat/moisture exhaust
  • Cross-ventilation: Vents on opposing walls
  • Draft-free: Air flow should be above birds, not on them
  • Secure: Cover all vents with 1/4" hardware cloth

We have two 12×4" vents near the roof on opposite walls, plus a ridge vent. Winter condensation disappeared after we added adequate ventilation.

4. Flooring

Three main options:

Dirt Floor:

  • Pros: Free, natural drainage, chickens can dust-bathe
  • Cons: Predators can dig under, harder to clean
  • Best for: Stationary coops with secure buried perimeter

Wood Floor:

  • Pros: Elevated design deters predators, easy to clean
  • Cons: Must be treated for moisture, can rot
  • Best for: Raised coops, mobile tractors

Concrete Slab:

  • Pros: Permanent, impervious to predators, easiest to clean
  • Cons: Most expensive, requires excavation
  • Best for: Large permanent coops

Our current coop has a concrete slab with 4" of sand—best decision we made. Cleaning is a breeze with a cat litter scoop.

Predator Protection

This is WHERE most DIY coops fail. Predators are relentless and clever.

Hardware Cloth, Not Chicken Wire

Chicken wire stops chickens from leaving. It does NOT stop predators from entering.

Use 1/4" or 1/2" hardware cloth everywhere:

  • All windows and vents
  • Entire run enclosure
  • Buried 12-18" underground around perimeter
  • Covering any gaps larger than 1/2"

Yes, hardware cloth is expensive ($1.50-2.00/sq ft). But it's far cheaper than losing your flock.

Secure Doors and Latches

Raccoons can open simple latches. Use:

  • Carabiners on hooks
  • Spring-loaded bolts
  • Pad locks for absolute security
  • Never rely on simple hook-and-eye latches

Run Coverage

Hawks and owls attack from above. Options:

  • Full roof: Best protection, more expensive
  • Netting: Cheap, works for most aerial predators
  • Wire top: Hardware cloth over top of run

We use a solid roof over half the run (shade) and hardware cloth over the other half (sun/rain).

Electric Fence (Optional but Effective)

A single strand of electric wire 6-8" off the ground around the run perimeter deters:

  • Foxes
  • Coyotes
  • Dogs
  • Raccoons

Solar electric fence chargers cost $60-120. Best investment for rural properties.

Step-by-Step Construction

Foundation (Day 1)

For permanent coop on concrete:

  1. Excavate 4" deep, 2 feet wider than coop footprint
  2. Lay landscape fabric
  3. Add 2" gravel base, compact
  4. Build 2×6 form, ensuring square and level
  5. Pour 4" concrete slab
  6. Let cure 3-5 days

For wood floor, build pier foundation with concrete blocks or treated 4×4 posts.

Framing (Day 2-3)

Standard 2×4 framing works well:

  1. Build floor frame if elevated
  2. Frame walls flat on ground, then stand up
  3. Use corner bracing for stability
  4. Frame roof—simple shed roof is easiest
  5. Install roof sheathing (OSB or plywood)

Pro tip: Build pop door (chicken entrance) large enough for you to reach through for cleaning (12×14" minimum).

Exterior (Day 4)

  1. Install windows (recycled works fine)
  2. Attach siding (T1-11, oriented strand board, or board-and-batten)
  3. Install roofing (metal, shingles, or corrugated panels)
  4. Paint or stain for weather protection

Interior (Day 5)

  1. Install roosting bars
  2. Build and mount nesting boxes
  3. Install droppings board under roosts (optional but recommended)
  4. Add bedding (pine shavings, straw, or sand)
  5. Install waterer and feeder

Run Construction (Day 6-7)

  1. Set corner posts (4×4 treated lumber)
  2. Attach top rail lumber
  3. Staple hardware cloth to frame
  4. Dig trench around perimeter
  5. Bury wire 12-18" deep, angled outward
  6. Backfill trench
  7. Install gate with secure latch
  8. Add roof structure or netting

Important Accessories

Automatic Door

Light-activated automatic door ($150-250) is life-changing:

  • Opens at sunrise, closes at dusk
  • No more predawn chicken chores
  • Protects flock if you're away overnight

We use a Chickcozy door—best $180 we've spent.

Droppings Board

Flat board under roosting bars catches 80% of manure:

  • Scrape daily (takes 2 minutes)
  • Keeps coop dramatically cleaner
  • Bedding lasts 10× longer

Dust Bath Area

Chickens need to dust bathe for pest control:

  • Designate corner of run
  • Mix: 50% sand, 25% wood ash, 25% diatomaceous earth
  • Keep dry if possible (roof)

Real-World Costs

Our 8×10 coop with 10×12 run breakdown:

  • Concrete slab (3 yards): $480
  • Lumber (2×4s, 4×4s, studs): $620
  • Siding and roofing: $340
  • Hardware cloth (150 sq ft): $285
  • Windows (recycled): $40
  • Roofing panels: $180
  • Hinges, latches, screws: $95
  • Paint/stain: $65
  • Automatic door: $180
  • Sand, bedding, misc: $120
  • Total: $2,405

This will house 12-15 chickens comfortably for 20+ years. Cost per chicken: $160-200.

Management and Maintenance

Daily Tasks

  • Collect eggs (morning and evening in summer)
  • Check water, refill if needed
  • Ensure feeder has food
  • Quick health check on flock
  • Scrape droppings board (if used)

Time: 5-10 minutes

Weekly Tasks

  • Thoroughly clean waterer
  • Refresh nesting box bedding
  • Check for and repair any damage

Time: 15-20 minutes

Monthly Tasks

  • Deep clean coop (remove all bedding)
  • Inspect for signs of pests or disease
  • Check hardware cloth for holes
  • Oil any squeaky hinges

Time: 1-2 hours

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Building too small

Everyone gets "chicken math" and ends up with more birds than planned. Build bigger than you think you need.

2. Poor ventilation

Respiratory issues, frostbite, and mold all stem from inadequate air flow.

3. Skimping on predator protection

Chicken wire, gaps in doors, or no buried perimeter—recipes for disaster.

4. No human access door

You need to get inside for cleaning. Make a door at least 2 feet wide.

5. Roosts lower than nesting boxes

Chickens sleep on the highest point. If that's nesting boxes, you get poop in your eggs.

6. Not considering winter

Frozen waterers, cold drafts, and snow blockades make winter tough. Plan for it during construction.

Mobile Tractor vs Stationary

Chicken Tractor (Mobile Coop)

Pros:

  • Fresh grass daily
  • Natural fertilization of land
  • Pest control for garden areas
  • No run necessary

Cons:

  • Must be lightweight (limited size)
  • Daily moving is labor-intensive
  • Can't use heavy permanent materials
  • Less predator protection

Stationary Coop (Our Choice)

Pros:

  • Better predator protection
  • More comfortable for chickens
  • Can be larger and sturdier
  • Less daily labor

Cons:

  • Run becomes a mud pit without management
  • Need to provide greens/treats
  • Waste management more intensive

Our Coop Evolution

Coop #1 (2017): Small ($300), poorly ventilated, chicken wire run. Lost birds to predators. Lessons learned.

Coop #2 (2019): Mid-sized ($1,200), better ventilation, hardware cloth. Good but cramped with 12 birds.

Coop #3 (2022): Current 8×10 design ($2,400). Perfect size, automatic door, concrete floor, exceptional predator protection. Plan to use for decades.

Final Thoughts

A well-designed coop makes chicken keeping a joy instead of a chore. Invest time in planning and don't skimp on predator protection. That initial investment in hardware cloth and proper construction will save you heartbreak and money in the long run.

Our chickens give us 8-10 fresh eggs daily, pest control, fantastic compost material, and endless entertainment. The coop pays for itself in saved grocery bills within 18-24 months.
