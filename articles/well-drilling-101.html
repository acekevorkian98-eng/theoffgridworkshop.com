Well Drilling 101: Access Your Own Water Source

📅 Published December 15, 2025 · ⏱️ 11 min read

Having your own water well is one of the most empowering steps toward true homestead independence. Our 285-foot drilled well has provided abundant, clean water for six years. This guide covers everything we learned about site selection, drilling methods, equipment, costs, and ongoing maintenance.

Before You Drill: Critical Considerations

Legal Requirements

Water rights laws vary dramatically by location:

  • Eastern US (Riparian Rights): Generally allows reasonable use of groundwater on your property
  • Western US (Prior Appropriation): Water rights may be separate from land ownership—research thoroughly
  • Permits: Most states require permits even for domestic wells. Expect $50-500 and 2-8 weeks processing
  • Setbacks: Minimum distances from septic systems (typically 50-100 feet), property lines, and structures

Critical: Violating water rights laws can result in fines, forced well closure, and even criminal charges. Always research local regulations first.

Hydrogeological Research

Before spending thousands on drilling, investigate water availability:

  • Ask neighbors: How deep are their wells? What's the yield? Any quality issues?
  • County well logs: Most states maintain public databases of existing well depths and yields
  • Geological surveys: USGS and state geological surveys publish aquifer maps
  • Water table depth: Shallower is cheaper to drill but may experience seasonal fluctuations

In our area, most wells are 200-350 feet deep, reaching a reliable sandstone aquifer. Our neighbors' wells provided crucial data that guided our decision.

Drilling Methods

1. Rotary Drilling (What We Used)

How it works: A drill bit rotates at the end of lengths of drill pipe. Drilling fluid (mud or water) circulates down the pipe, cools the bit, and carries cuttings to the surface.

Advantages:

  • Can drill through nearly any formation (rock, clay, sand)
  • Fastest method for deep wells (200+ feet)
  • Most common—easy to find contractors

Disadvantages:

  • Most expensive method
  • Requires professional equipment and expertise
  • Not practical for DIY

2. Cable Tool Drilling

An older technique using a heavy drill bit that repeatedly pounds and pulverizes rock.

Advantages:

  • Simpler equipment
  • More reliable water quality testing during drilling
  • Good for fractured rock formations

Disadvantages:

  • Very slow (10-30 feet per day)
  • Harder to find contractors with equipment
  • Not suitable for unconsolidated formations

3. Driven Point Wells (Shallow Wells Only)

A pointed screened pipe is literally hammered into shallow water-bearing sand or gravel.

Advantages:

  • DIY-friendly
  • Very inexpensive ($200-800)
  • Can be installed in a weekend

Disadvantages:

  • Limited to 25 feet depth maximum
  • Only works in sandy/gravelly soil (not rock)
  • Vulnerable to contamination from surface water
  • May run dry during droughts

The Drilling Process: What to Expect

Day 1: Site Preparation and Setup

  • Drill truck arrives and positions over drilling site
  • Crew sets up mud pit and mixing system
  • First few feet drilled to establish hole
  • Drilling typically begins in earnest

Day 2-3: Active Drilling

Our 285-foot well took 2.5 days to drill. The process:

  1. Drill continuously, adding pipe sections as depth increases
  2. Monitor drilling speed (changes indicate different geological layers)
  3. Test water yield at various depths
  4. Stop when adequate water is found in suitable formation

We hit good water at 265 feet but drilled an additional 20 feet into the aquifer for extra capacity and filtering.

Day 4: Well Completion

  • Install well casing (steel or PVC pipe)
  • Set well screen at water-bearing zone
  • Grout annular space (gap between casing and borehole)
  • Install sanitary well cap
  • Development pumping (removes drilling mud and sediment)

Well Components

Casing

The main pipe that lines the well bore. Our well uses 6-inch diameter steel casing from surface down to the bedrock, then PVC casing through the aquifer.

Well Screen

Perforated section of casing that allows water in while keeping sand and gravel out. Located at the water-bearing zone.

Grout/Seal

Cement or bentonite clay mixture that fills the space between casing and the bore hole, preventing surface contamination from seeping down the outside of the casing.

Pitless Adapter (Critical in Freezing Climates)

Allows the pipe to exit the casing below the frost line, routing water horizontally to your house underground. This prevents freezing and eliminates the need for a well house.

Pump Selection

Submersible Pumps (Our Choice)

Pump sits at the bottom of the well, submerged in water.

Advantages:

  • Can lift water from any depth
  • Quiet (pump is underground)
  • Protected from weather and freezing
  • Prime automatically

Disadvantages:

  • More expensive ($800-2,500)
  • Requires pulling entire pump for repairs
  • Shorter lifespan than jet pumps (10-15 years)

We use a 3/4 HP Grundfos submersible pump set at 240 feet depth. It delivers 10 GPM at 50 PSI—more than adequate for household needs.

Jet Pumps (Shallow Wells)

Surface-mounted pump that uses suction to pull water up.

Limitations:

  • Maximum practical depth: 25 feet (shallow jet) or 120 feet (deep jet with ejector package)
  • Noisy operation
  • Requires priming
  • Must be protected from freezing

Water Quality Testing

We sent water samples to a certified lab within one week of well completion. Tests included:

Basic Package ($150)

  • Total coliform and E. coli bacteria
  • pH
  • Hardness (dissolved minerals)
  • Iron and manganese
  • Nitrates

Extended Package ($400 - Optional)

  • Heavy metals (lead, arsenic, mercury)
  • Organic chemicals and pesticides
  • Radon
  • Uranium (in granite regions)

Our results:

  • Bacteria: None detected ✓
  • pH: 7.2 (neutral) ✓
  • Hardness: 180 ppm (moderately hard)
  • Iron: 0.8 ppm (slightly elevated but acceptable)
  • All other parameters: Within EPA limits ✓

We installed a simple sediment filter and UV sterilizer for drinking water—total cost $450.

Real Costs: Our Complete Breakdown

  • Well drilling (285 feet @ $28/foot): $7,980
  • 6" steel casing: Included
  • Grouting and well cap: Included
  • Submersible pump and control box: $1,850
  • Pitless adapter: $185
  • Electrical work (220V circuit to well): $650
  • Pressure tank (120 gallon): $480
  • Trenching and pipe to house (320 feet): $1,200
  • Water testing: $150
  • UV filter and sediment filter: $450
  • Permits and misc supplies: $380
  • TOTAL: $13,325

Drilling costs vary widely by region ($15-$65/foot). Always get multiple quotes and verify contractors are licensed and insured.

Ongoing Maintenance

Annual Tasks

  • Test water quality (bacteria test minimum)
  • Check pressure tank air pressure
  • Inspect well cap and seal for damage
  • Verify electrical connections are tight
  • Replace sediment filters

Every 3-5 Years

  • Comprehensive water quality testing
  • Inspect and clean pitless adapter
  • Have well yield tested

Every 10-15 Years

  • Replace UV sterilizer bulb and housing
  • Consider pull pump for inspection
  • Expect eventual submersible pump replacement

Our annual maintenance costs average $200 (water testing, filters, minor parts).

Red Flags When Hiring a Driller

  • No license/insurance: Walk away immediately
  • "We'll find water for sure": Honest drillers acknowledge uncertainty
  • Refusing to provide references: Always ask for recent customer contacts
  • Unusually cheap quotes: Quality drilling isn't cheap
  • Pressure to sign immediately: Take time to review contracts
  • No written contract: Must have detailed agreement specifying depth, diameter, completion details

When a Well Makes Sense

A well is a great investment if you:

  • Have property with no municipal water access
  • Plan to live on the property long-term (10+ years)
  • Have confirmed groundwater availability in your area
  • Can afford the $8,000-$20,000 upfront investment
  • Want true water independence

Consider alternatives if you:

  • Are in an area with known water scarcity
  • Have complex water rights restrictions
  • Only need water temporarily or seasonally
  • Can access affordable municipal water

Our Experience After 6 Years

Our well has been nearly maintenance-free. The pump runs reliably, water quality remains excellent, and yield has never diminished even during droughts. The peace of mind knowing we have unlimited clean water—independent of any utility—is priceless.

Total 6-year costs beyond the initial $13,325:

  • Electricity (pump operation): ~$180/year
  • Filters and testing: ~$200/year
  • Total annual operating cost: $380

Compared to hauling water or paying for municipal service, our well paid for itself in about 7 years.

Final Advice

Drilling a well is a significant investment, but it's one of the most valuable improvements you can make to rural property. Do your homework, hire reputable contractors, don't cut corners on quality, and test your water thoroughly.

Having your own water source transforms your homestead from dependent to independent. It's freedom, security, and peace of mind—all flowing from deep beneath your feet.
