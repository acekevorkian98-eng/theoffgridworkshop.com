Battery Bank Sizing Guide

📅 Published November 28, 2025 · ⏱️ 11 min read

Sizing a battery bank is one of the most critical—and most misunderstood—aspects of off-grid power systems. Too small, and you'll experience frequent low-battery cutoffs. Too large, and you've wasted thousands of dollars. This guide covers the calculations, chemistry comparisons, and real-world considerations from our experience building and upgrading multiple battery banks.

Why Proper Sizing Matters

Your battery bank serves three essential functions:

  • Energy storage: Stores power from solar/wind/hydro for use at night or during low production
  • Load buffering: Handles surge loads (pump starting, power tools) that exceed generation capacity
  • System stability: Provides stable voltage for inverters and electronics

Under-sizing leads to daily deep discharges, which dramatically shorten battery life. Over-sizing wastes money and can actually harm batteries that need regular cycling.

Basic Sizing Formula

Start with your daily energy consumption in watt-hours (Wh):

Battery Capacity = (Daily Wh × Days Autonomy) / (Depth of Discharge × Efficiency)

Example Calculation:

  • Daily consumption: 6,000 Wh
  • Days autonomy desired: 3 days
  • Depth of discharge (lithium): 80%
  • System efficiency: 90%

Calculation:

(6,000 × 3) / (0.80 × 0.90) = 25,000 Wh = 25 kWh battery

Understanding Key Terms

Days of Autonomy

How many days can your battery sustain loads without charging? Typical values:

  • 1-2 days: Grid-tie with battery backup systems
  • 2-3 days: Off-grid with reliable solar or wind
  • 3-5 days: Off-grid with backup generator
  • 5-7 days: Remote locations, harsh winters

We designed for 3 days in summer, effectively 4-5 days in winter due to larger array and reduced consumption.

Depth of Discharge (DOD)

How much of battery capacity you actually use:

  • Flooded Lead-Acid: 50% max (discharging deeper significantly shortens life)
  • AGM Lead-Acid: 50-60% max
  • Lithium LiFePO4: 80-90% usable (some systems allow 100%)

Critical: If you need 10kWh usable with lead-acid batteries, you must install 20kWh total capacity!

Voltage Selection

Common system voltages:

  • 12V: Small systems \<1kW, RVs, boats. Efficient for low power.
  • 24V: Mid-sized 1-3kW systems. Good balance of availability and efficiency.
  • 48V: Systems \>3kW. Most efficient, reduced wire gauge requirements.
  • Higher: Some lithium systems run 300-400V DC

We started with 24V lead-acid, upgraded to 48V lithium. The higher voltage dramatically reduced wire losses.

Battery Chemistry Comparison

Flooded Lead-Acid (FLA)

Advantages:

  • Lowest upfront cost ($150-300/kWh)
  • Well-understood, proven technology
  • Tolerates overcharging fairly well
  • Readily available, DIY-friendly

Disadvantages:

  • Requires monthly watering and equalization
  • Off-gasses hydrogen (ventilation required)
  • Only 50% usable capacity
  • Heavy (65 lbs per kwh)
  • Shorter lifespan (5-8 years)
  • Must stay above freezing when discharged

Best for: Budget-constrained initial systems, those comfortable with maintenance

AGM (Absorbed Glass Mat) Lead-Acid

Advantages:

  • No maintenance, sealed design
  • Can install in living space
  • Better cycle life than flooded
  • Lower self-discharge

Disadvantages:

  • Higher cost than flooded ($250-400/kWh)
  • Intolerant of overcharging
  • Still only 50% usable
  • Shorter lifespan than lithium (7-10 years)

Best for: Those wanting sealed batteries but not ready for lithium cost

Lithium LiFePO4 (Lithium Iron Phosphate)

Advantages:

  • 80-90% usable capacity
  • 10-15 year lifespan (3000-5000 cycles)
  • Lightweight (20 lbs per kWh)
  • Fast charging capability
  • Excellent low-temperature performance
  • No maintenance
  • Built-in battery management system (BMS)

Disadvantages:

  • Higher upfront cost ($400-700/kWh)
  • Requires compatible charge controller
  • Cannot charge below freezing (needs heating)
  • More complex BMS can fail

Best for: Long-term installations, those who can afford initial investment

Total Cost of Ownership

The real story emerges over time. For a 20kWh usable capacity system:

Flooded Lead-Acid:

  • Need 40kWh total (50% DOD) = $8,000 initial
  • Replacement every 7 years = $8,000 again
  • 15-year total: $16,000 + maintenance time
  • Cost per kWh-cycle: $0.53

Lithium LiFePO4:

  • Need 25kWh total (80% DOD) = $12,500 initial
  • Likely no replacement in 15 years
  • 15-year total: $12,500
  • Cost per kWh-cycle: $0.17

Lithium is significantly cheaper over lifespan and requires zero maintenance!

Detailed Sizing Worksheet

Step 1: Calculate Daily Consumption

List all loads and hours of daily use:

Refrigerator: 150W × 8 hrs = 1,200 Wh
Lights: 60W × 5 hrs = 300 Wh
Laptop: 65W × 6 hrs = 390 Wh
TV: 100W × 3 hrs = 300 Wh
Water pump: 750W × 0.5 hrs = 375 Wh
Misc: 500 Wh

Total: 3,065 Wh/day
Add 20% for inverter losses: 3,678 Wh/day

Step 2: Choose Days of Autonomy

Consider your climate and backup options. We'll use 3 days for this example.

Step 3: Select Battery Chemistry

This determines usable capacity. Let's compare both:

Lead-Acid (50% DOD):

(3,678 Wh × 3 days) / 0.50 = 22,068 Wh = 22 kWh battery bank

Lithium (80% DOD):

(3,678 Wh × 3 days) / 0.80 = 13,793 Wh = 14 kWh battery bank

Step 4: Convert to Amp-Hours

Batteries are rated in Ah. Convert based on system voltage:

Amp-Hours = Watt-Hours / System Voltage

For 14kWh lithium at 48V:

14,000 Wh / 48V = 292 Ah @ 48V

Wiring Configurations

Series vs Parallel

Series: Adds voltage, keeps Ah same

  • Two 12V 200Ah batteries in series = 24V 200Ah
  • Four 12V 200Ah in series = 48V 200Ah

Parallel: Adds Ah, keeps voltage same

  • Two 12V 200Ah batteries in parallel = 12V 400Ah

Series-Parallel: Increases both

  • Four 12V 200Ah: two parallel strings of two series = 24V 400Ah

Our 48V Lithium Bank

Configuration:

  • Sixteen 3.2V 100Ah LiFePO4 cells
  • Wired in series: 16 × 3.2V = 51.2V nominal
  • Capacity: 100Ah @ 51.2V = 5.12 kWh per string
  • Three strings in parallel = 15.36 kWh total

This provides ~12kWh usable at 80% DOD—perfect for our 3-day needs.

Critical Installation Considerations

Cable Sizing

Undersized cables cause voltage drop and heat. For battery connections:

  • 12V systems: Use 2/0 AWG or larger for main battery cables
  • 24V systems: Use 2 AWG minimum
  • 48V systems: Can use 4 AWG for most applications

Rule: Battery cables should be as short as possible. Each foot adds resistance.

Location and Ventilation

Temperature: Batteries perform best at 70-80°F

  • Cold reduces capacity (50% loss at 0°F for lead-acid)
  • Heat accelerates aging
  • Install in temper-controlled space if possible

For Lead-Acid:

  • Must ventilate to outside (hydrogen gas)
  • Keep away from ignition sources
  • Install in non-living space
  • Acid-resistant flooring recommended

For Lithium:

  • Can install in living areas
  • Still want ventilation for heat management
  • Some heating required if charging below 32°F

Fusing and Protection

Every positive connection must be fused near the battery:

  • Battery-to-inverter: 1.25× max inverter current
  • Charge controller to battery: 1.25× max charge current
  • Between parallel batteries: Individual fuses on each

We use ANL fuses (automotive-style) for main connections, easier to replace than breakers.

Monitoring and Management

Essential Monitoring

At minimum, monitor:

  • State of Charge (SOC): Percentage of capacity remaining
  • Voltage: Real-time battery voltage
  • Current: Charging and discharge rates
  • Temperature: Battery temperature

We use a Victron BMV-712 battery monitor ($235). Worth every penny for peace of mind.

Battery Management Systems (BMS)

Lithium batteries REQUIRE a BMS to prevent:

  • Overcharging (causes fire risk)
  • Over-discharging (permanent damage)
  • Cell imbalance (capacity loss)
  • Thermal runaway

Quality lithium batteries have BMS built-in. DIY lithium builds need external BMS ($200-800).

Maintenance

Flooded Lead-Acid

  • Monthly: Check water levels, add distilled water
  • Quarterly: Check specific gravity with hydrometer
  • Biannually: Equalization charge (controlled overcharge)
  • Annually: Clean terminals, check connections

Lithium

  • Quarterly: Verify BMS operation
  • Annually: Clean terminals, check connections
  • That's it! No watering, no equalization

Common Sizing Mistakes

1. Ignoring inverter efficiency:

Don't forget 10-15% losses converting DC to AC. Always add 20% to consumption calculations.

2. Using nameplate capacity:

A "10kWh" lead-acid battery is NOT 10kWh usable. Remember the 50% DOD!

3. Mismatched batteries:

Never mix old and new batteries. The old weak ones drag down the whole bank.

4. Inadequate charge rate:

Your solar/wind must be able to fully recharge batteries. Minimum 10% of bank Ah rating.

5. Forgetting temperature derating:

Batteries lose capacity in cold. Size 20-30% larger if installing in unheated space.

Our Upgrade Journey

Original System (2018):

  • Eight 6V FLA golf cart batteries
  • Wired for 24V, 900Ah (21.6 kWh total, 10.8 kWh usable)
  • Cost: $1,600
  • Lifespan: 6.5 years

Current System (2024):

  • Three 48V 100Ah lithium modules
  • 15.4 kWh total, 12.3 kWh usable
  • Cost: $8,900
  • Expected lifespan: 12-15 years

The lithium upgrade was transformative:

  • More usable capacity despite smaller total size
  • Zero maintenance (no more watering)
  • Faster charging (accepts higher current)
  • Better voltage stability
  • 600 lbs lighter!

Final Thoughts

Battery bank sizing is part science, part art. The formulas provide a starting point, but your actual needs depend on:

  • Climate and seasonal variation
  • Backup power availability
  • Load flexibility
  • Budget constraints
  • Future expansion plans

Start conservatively—it's easier to add capacity later than to deal with premature battery failure from under-sizing. And seriously consider lithium if you can swing the upfront cost. The long-term economics and reduced hassle make it a no-brainer for permanent installations.
