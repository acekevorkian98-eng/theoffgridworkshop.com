Building a Year-Round Greenhouse

📅 Published January 5, 2026 · ⏱️ 15 min read

Fresh vegetables in February—that was our goal when we started planning our greenhouse six years ago. At 8,500 feet elevation in Colorado, our outdoor growing season is a mere 90 days. Building a proper year-round greenhouse extended that to 365 days and transformed our food security. Here's everything we learned.

Why Year-Round Matters

Most greenhouses are glorified season extenders—they add a few weeks in spring and fall. A true year-round greenhouse maintains productive temperatures even during harsh winters without fossil fuel heating. This requires thoughtful design around four key principles:

  • Maximum solar gain during winter
  • Thermal mass for heat storage
  • Superior insulation where it counts
  • Proper ventilation for summer cooling

Sizing Your Greenhouse

Our greenhouse is 16×24 feet (384 sq ft). This size provides enough growing space to supply vegetables for two people year-round while remaining manageable for one person to maintain. General sizing guidelines:

  • 100-200 sq ft: Supplemental production, herbs, starts
  • 200-400 sq ft: Substantial production for 2-4 people
  • 400+ sq ft: Commercial-scale or large family needs

Remember: a well-managed small greenhouse outproduces a poorly managed large one.

Orientation and Site Selection

East-West vs North-South

This is crucial and often misunderstood. Orient your greenhouse with the long axis running EAST-WEST. This maximizes southern exposure for winter sun, which is your primary free heat source.

Site Considerations

  • Full southern sun exposure (no shade from 9am-3pm)
  • Protection from cold north winds if possible
  • Level ground or easily leveled
  • Close to water source
  • Good drainage (avoid low spots)

The Insulated North Wall

Here's our most important design decision: the north wall is NOT glazed—it's heavily insulated instead. This approach:

  • Reduces heat loss by 40% compared to all-glass designs
  • Provides a reflective surface to bounce light onto plants
  • Creates space for thermal mass and storage
  • Significantly improves winter performance

We built our north wall with 2×6 framing, R-19 insulation, and painted the interior white. Total additional cost: $300. Value in heat retention: priceless.

Glazing Selection

Your glazing choice dramatically affects both performance and cost:

Polycarbonate (What We Used)

  • Pros: Excellent insulation (R-1.7 for twin-wall, R-2.5 for triple-wall), diffuses light evenly, virtually indestructible, affordable
  • Cons: Less aesthetically pleasing than glass, degrades in UV over 10-15 years
  • Cost: ~$2.50-4.50 per sq ft for twin-wall

Glass

  • Pros: Beautiful, lasts forever, maximum light transmission
  • Cons: Poor insulation (R-0.9 single-pane), breaks easily, expensive, heavy
  • Cost: ~$8-15 per sq ft for double-pane

We chose 8mm twin-wall polycarbonate for the south, east, and west walls. Six years later, it's still performing excellently.

Thermal Mass: The Secret to Winter Success

Thermal mass absorbs heat during sunny days and releases it at night, moderating temperature swings. Without it, your greenhouse will overheat during the day and freeze at night.

Our Thermal Mass System

  • Water barrels: 16× 55-gallon drums painted black, arranged along north wall
  • Gravel floor: 4 inches of dark gravel over landscape fabric
  • Raised beds: Soil itself provides mass

The water barrels alone store about 880 gallons (7,300 pounds) of water. When sunny day temperatures hit 75°F, this mass absorbs enormous amounts of heat and releases it overnight. Our winter nighttime lows rarely drop below 40°F inside, even when it's 10°F outside.

Ventilation System

Winter heat retention is only half the battle. Summer cooling is equally critical, and inadequate ventilation will literally cook your plants.

Passive Ventilation

  • Automatic vent openers on ridge vents (wax cylinder actuators, no electricity needed)
  • Low vents on east wall for cross-breeze
  • Rule of thumb: ventilation area should equal 15-20% of floor area

Active Ventilation

We added a solar-powered exhaust fan for extreme summer days. It kicks on via thermostat at 85°F and makes a huge difference during heat waves.

Foundation and Framing

We built on a pressure-treated 4×4 base sitting on concrete pier blocks. The structure is simple 2×4 framing on 24-inch centers—nothing fancy, but it's survived 100mph winds and 4 feet of snow.

Key Structural Points

  • Use pressure-treated lumber for base and any ground contact
  • 16-inch on-center spacing for roofs in snow country
  • Roof pitch of 6/12 or steeper for snow shedding and winter sun angle
  • Anchor securely—greenhouses are lightweight and wind-vulnerable

Growing Systems

We use a combination of in-ground raised beds and container growing:

  • Winter: Cold-hardy greens (kale, spinach, lettuce, chard), root vegetables in deep beds
  • Spring/Fall: Transition crops, broccoli, peas
  • Summer: Tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, herbs

With succession planting, we harvest something almost every day year-round.

Heating Backup

We designed to not need supplemental heat, but we keep a small propane heater as emergency backup for extreme cold snaps (below -20°F). We've used it perhaps 5 nights total in 6 years.

Real Costs

Our 16×24 greenhouse total costs:

  • Lumber and framing: $1,200
  • Polycarbonate glazing: $950
  • Water barrels (used): $200
  • Automatic vent openers: $120
  • Solar exhaust fan: $180
  • Gravel, soil, amendments: $400
  • Miscellaneous (screws, caulk, paint): $250
  • Total: $3,300

Divide by 6 years of use and we're at $550/year for year-round vegetables. That's paid for itself many times over.

Lessons Learned

  1. Thermal mass is non-negotiable for true year-round growing
  2. The insulated north wall was our best design decision
  3. Ventilation prevents more problems than heating
  4. Start with soil quality—your structure is only as good as what you grow in
  5. Keep it simple—complex automated systems break down

Final Thoughts

Building our greenhouse was one of the most rewarding projects we've undertaken. The ability to harvest fresh spinach in January, start seedlings in February, and grow tomatoes through October has fundamentally changed how we eat and our degree of food security.

Whether you're in a harsh climate like us or just want to extend your season, a well-designed greenhouse is worth every hour and dollar invested. Questions about greenhouse building? Drop us a line!
