Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to go completely off-grid?

It depends heavily on your lifestyle and starting point. A minimal off-grid setup — small cabin, basic solar, rainwater collection, and a productive garden — can be built for $5,000–$15,000 over time if you do most of the work yourself. A full off-grid home with modern comforts (refrigeration, power tools, hot water, etc.) typically costs $25,000–$100,000+. The single biggest expense is usually the solar and battery system.

What size solar system do I need to live off-grid?

Start with a power audit. Most minimally-equipped off-grid households (fridge, lights, fans, phone/laptop charging, water pump) consume 1,000–3,000Wh per day. A 1,000W solar array with a 200Ah LiFePO4 battery bank handles this in most climates. Larger households with power tools, air conditioning, or electric cooking need 3,000–10,000W+ of solar. Use our solar sizing guide for exact calculations.

Is it legal to collect rainwater and live off-grid?

Rainwater collection is legal in most US states, though some have restrictions on collection volume. Full off-grid living is legal in most rural areas, though some counties require permits for structures, septic/composting toilet systems, and minimum dwelling sizes. Always check your local zoning laws before purchasing land. Our rainwater guide covers legality by state.

What’s the hardest part of living off-grid?

Most experienced off-gridders say it’s not the technology — it’s the mindset shift. Off-grid life requires constant attentiveness to your systems (battery state, water levels, food stores) and a willingness to live within your resource limits. The second hardest challenge is usually water in dry climates and heating in cold ones. Power systems, once properly designed, tend to be the least problematic.

Do I need to be a DIYer to go off-grid?

No, but it helps enormously. You can hire out most of the installation work for your solar system, water system, and shelter — but you’ll pay significantly more, and you’ll be dependent on contractors for repairs. Most successful off-gridders develop basic skills in electrical work (12V DC systems), plumbing, and carpentry over time. Starting with good guides and community forums makes the learning curve manageable.

Which battery is best for off-grid solar: lithium or lead-acid?

LiFePO4 (lithium iron phosphate) is the clear winner for most off-grid applications despite the higher upfront cost. It has 2,000–5,000 charge cycles vs. 300–500 for flooded lead-acid, can be discharged to 80–90% without damage vs. 50% for lead-acid, is maintenance-free, and has a much better energy density. Over a 10-year period, LiFePO4 typically costs less per usable kWh than lead-acid replacements.
